How this shows up

I look for the problem underneath the request.

For Eventbrite, interviews showed the issue was not just event discovery — it was retention and trust. For PocketPlans, users did not want more venue options; they wanted a faster way to decide where to go.

100+user conversations shaped

How this shows up

I simplify flows until the next action feels obvious.

At BVM, simplifying complex grant workflows reduced staff training time by 85%. In PocketPlans, cutting the experience from 10 inputs to 4 steps helped users reach a decision faster instead of getting stuck in setup.

85%training time reduced through
clearer workflows
How this shows up

I use prototypes to make tradeoffs visible.

For Eventbrite and Worldify, Figma prototypes helped turn research insights into testable product directions. Prototyping made it easier to discuss what mattered: the flow, the priority, and the user behavior we wanted to change.

2award-winning prototypes built
from research-backed decisions
How this shows up

I use AI when it earns trust.

On PocketPlans, I chose API-driven filtering over LLM generation because hallucinated venues would break user trust. On Structify, I used LLMs because the output structure was predictable enough to automate reliably.

How this shows up

I do not stop at launch.

On PocketPlans, Microsoft Clarity revealed a 26.67% dead click rate that users never mentioned in feedback. That signal shaped v2 priorities around affordances, interactivity, and retention loops.

At BVM, I moved from user flows and accessibility checks to PRDs, acceptance criteria, and engineering-ready specs. That bridge between user need, design detail, and implementation is where my PM and UX strengths meet.

About

The person behind the process.

I started out building things as an engineer. In undergrad, I built a gesture-controlled laptop using Arduino and an ultrasound sensor, and it made me wonder: what happens when we rethink how people interact with technology?

That curiosity took me from Electronics and Communication Engineering to Engineering Management at Northeastern, where I learned to look beyond the technology and understand the people using it.

Since then, I’ve built AI-powered products, conducted user research, and worked across product strategy and design to turn ideas into intuitive experiences. The engineer in me wants things to work. The designer in me wants them to feel effortless. The product thinker in me wants them to matter.

I grew up in a home full of music, with a mum who teaches and plays the veena, a classical South Indian string instrument. Watching her taught me that creativity is equal parts structure, patience, and feeling. Something I carry into the way I solve problems.

Outside of work, I’m usually taking photos of small details, eating my way through a new city, playing the veena when I’m back home, or sketching on Procreate. Reminders to stay curious, observant, and open to being a beginner.
